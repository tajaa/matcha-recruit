## Manufacturing

### process_safety

#### WSH Major Hazard Installations (MHI) Registration
- **regulation_key**: `mhi_registration`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: WSH (Major Hazard Installations) Regulations 2017
- **description**: Factories that process, handle, or store dangerous substances at or above prescribed threshold quantities must register as a Major Hazard Installation (MHI) with the Ministry of Manpower (MOM). MHI registration replaces the standard factory registration under the WSH (Registration of Factories) Regulations. Registration must be submitted at least 6 months before operations commence, via the LicenceOne government portal.
- **current_value**: MHI registration required for installations handling dangerous substances above threshold quantities; registration processing takes ~30 working days
- **source_url**: https://www.mom.gov.sg/workplace-safety-and-health/major-hazard-installations
- **source_name**: Ministry of Manpower (MOM)
- **requires_written_policy**: true

#### MHI Safety Case Regime
- **regulation_key**: `mhi_safety_case`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: Safety Case Submission and Review (WSH MHI Regulations 2017)
- **description**: Occupiers of Major Hazard Installations are required to prepare and submit a Safety Case report demonstrating that risk reduction measures are effective, reliable, and that risks of major hazard accidents have been reduced to ALARP (As Low as Reasonably Practicable). The Safety Case must be submitted for assessment once every five years. The report must cover process safety management systems, hazard identification, risk assessment, and emergency planning.
- **current_value**: Safety Case submission required every 5 years; must demonstrate ALARP risk reduction
- **source_url**: https://sso.agc.gov.sg/SL/WSHA2006-S202-2017
- **source_name**: Singapore Statutes Online
- **requires_written_policy**: true

#### Factory Notification and Registration
- **regulation_key**: `factory_registration`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: WSH (Registration of Factories) Regulations — Factory Notification or Registration
- **description**: All workplaces defined as a "factory" under the WSH Act must either notify or register with MOM depending on the risk level of activities. High-risk activities (e.g., shipyards, wafer fabrication, pharmaceutical manufacturing, metalworking factories with >100 employees, refineries, petrochemical plants, bulk storage terminals) require a Certificate of Registration. Low-risk activities require a one-time Factory Notification. Operating without notification or registration is punishable by a fine of up to SGD 5,000.
- **current_value**: High-risk factories require Certificate of Registration; low-risk require Factory Notification; SGD 5,000 fine for non-compliance
- **source_url**: https://www.mom.gov.sg/workplace-safety-and-health/factory-notification-and-registration/requirements-for-factories

#### Process Hazard Analysis (PHA)
- **regulation_key**: `process_hazard_analysis`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: WSH Guidelines on Process Hazard Analysis
- **description**: Singapore's Workplace Safety and Health Council (WSHC) publishes guidelines on Process Hazard Analysis (PHA) for manufacturing facilities handling hazardous materials. While not identical to US OSHA PSM, the guidelines require systematic identification and evaluation of process hazards using methods such as HAZOP, What-If, Fault Tree Analysis, and similar techniques. ### environmental_compliance

#### EPMA Pollution Control Study (PCS)
- **regulation_key**: `pollution_control_study`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: Environmental Protection and Management Act (EPMA) — Pollution Control Study Requirement
- **description**: Under the EPMA, all new manufacturing facilities must complete and submit a Pollution Control Study (PCS) to the National Environment Agency (NEA) as part of the application for Written Permission before construction begins. The PCS is a comprehensive report detailing all potential sources of pollution (air emissions, water discharge, noise, waste) and the control measures to be implemented. It must be prepared by a competent person, typically an environmental engineering consultant. NEA updated its PCS Guidelines in August 2025.
- **current_value**: PCS required before construction approval; must cover air, water, noise, and waste pollution sources; prepared by competent environmental consultant
- **source_url**: https://www.nea.gov.sg/docs/default-source/our-services/building-planning/pcs-guidelines-19-aug-25.pdf

### chemical_safety

- **regulation_key**: `hazardous_substances_licence`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: EPMA — Hazardous Substances Licensing Scheme
- **description**: Under the EPMA, companies that import, export, manufacture, sell, transport, store, or use regulated hazardous substances must obtain a Hazardous Substances (HS) Licence from the NEA. Companies that only purchase, store, or use smaller quantities may apply for an HS Permit instead. ### industrial_hygiene

#### Workplace Noise Exposure Limits — WSH (Noise) Regulations 2011
- **regulation_key**: `noise_exposure_limits`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: WSH (Noise) Regulations 2011 — Permissible Exposure Limits and Hearing Conservation
- **description**: The WSH (Noise) Regulations 2011 establish permissible exposure limits (PELs) for workplace noise to prevent noise-induced deafness (NID). All continuous, impulsive, or impact noise from 80 dB(A) to 140 dB(A) must be included in noise exposure computations. The PEL is exceeded if a worker's exposure at a specific sound level surpasses the corresponding allowable duration. Manufacturing is the top contributor to NID cases in Singapore — 7 out of 10 metalworking workplaces exceed 90 dB(A). Employers must implement a Hearing Conservation Programme when noise exceeds 85 dB(A).
- **current_value**: PEL based on noise level/duration schedule (80-140 dB(A) range); Hearing Conservation Programme mandatory above 85 dB(A); manufacturing is #1 contributor to NID
- **source_url**: https://sso.agc.gov.sg/SL/WSHA2006-S424-2011
- **source_name**: Singapore Statutes Online
- **requires_written_policy**: true

### minimum_wage

#### Progressive Wage Model (PWM)
- **regulation_key**: `progressive_wage_model`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: Progressive Wage Model (PWM) — Sector-Specific Minimum Wages
- **description**: Singapore does not have a universal national minimum wage. Instead, it uses the Progressive Wage Model (PWM), which prescribes minimum basic monthly salaries for specific sectors including cleaning, security, food services, waste management, retail, and specific occupational roles (administrators, drivers). The PWM charts a wage ladder where each rung corresponds to higher skills, training, and job responsibilities. From 1 July 2025 to 30 June 2026, entry-level minimum wages increase to SGD 1,910–2,325/month depending on sector. Government co-funds wage increases at 40% in 2025 and additional 20% in 2026.

### overtime

#### Employment Act Part IV — Overtime and Working Hours
- **regulation_key**: `employment_act_overtime`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: Employment Act Part IV — Hours of Work, Overtime, and Rest Days
- **description**: Part IV of the Employment Act governs overtime for workmen earning up to SGD 4,500/month and non-workmen earning up to SGD 2,600/month (excluding managers and executives). Standard working hours are capped at 44 hours/week and 8 hours/day (9 hours if working fewer than 5 days/week). Overtime must be paid at least 1.5x the hourly basic rate. ### meal_breaks

#### Meal Break and Rest Period Requirements
- **regulation_key**: `meal_break_requirements`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: Singapore
- **title**: Employment Act — Meal Breaks and Rest Periods
- **description**: Employees who work continuously for more than 6 hours must be given a break. If continuous work of 8 hours is required, breaks of at least 45 minutes must be provided for meals. Employees are entitled to a minimum rest period of 11 consecutive hours between the end of one workday and the start of the next. At least 1 rest day per week (continuous 24 hours, typically Sunday) is required. For shift workers, the rest day can extend to 30 continuous hours. Note: the statutory meal/rest break rights apply only to employees earning less than SGD 2,500/month.
