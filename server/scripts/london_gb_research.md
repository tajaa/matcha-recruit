### overtime

#### Working Time Regulations — Maximum Hours
- **regulation_key**: `daily_weekly_overtime`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: United Kingdom
- **title**: UK Maximum Working Hours (Working Time Regulations 1998)
- **description**: Maximum 48-hour average working week, calculated over a rolling 17-week reference period (extendable to 26 weeks by collective agreement). Workers may voluntarily sign a written opt-out to work beyond 48 hours; the opt-out must be separate from the employment contract, and workers can cancel it with up to 3 months' notice (default 7 days). Employers must not cause detriment to workers who refuse to opt out. The UK has NO statutory overtime pay premium — there is no legal requirement to pay time-and-a-half or any enhanced rate for overtime. However, average hourly pay must not fall below NMW. Regular overtime must be included in holiday pay calculations per Bear Scotland v Fulton [2015]. Exempt workers include armed forces and managing executives with autonomous decision-making.
- **current_value**: 48 hours/week average (17-week reference period)
- **numeric_value**: 48
- **effective_date**: 1998-10-01
- **source_url**: https://www.acas.org.uk/working-time-rules/the-48-hour-weekly-maximum
- **source_name**: Acas
- **requires_written_policy**: false

### sick_leave

- **regulation_key**: `statutory_sick_leave`
- **jurisdiction_level**: national
- **jurisdiction_name**: United Kingdom
- **title**: UK Statutory Sick Pay (SSP)
- **description**: Employers must pay SSP to eligible employees. 2025/26: £118.75/week for up to 28 weeks. First 3 qualifying days are unpaid ("waiting days"). - **title**: UK Working Time and Scheduling Rules
- **description**: The UK has no predictive scheduling law equivalent to US ordinances.
