## General Labor

### Minimum Wage
- **Category**: `minimum_wage`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: $16.90/hr (general); no separate tipped wage (CA requires full minimum wage for tipped employees); exempt salary threshold $70,304/yr ($1,352/week)
- **Effective Date**: 2026-01-01
- **Source**: [CA DIR Minimum Wage](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/minimum_wage.htm); [DIR News Release 2025-118](https://www.dir.ca.gov/DIRNews/2025/2025-118.html)
- **Details**: California minimum wage increased from $16.50 to $16.90/hr effective January 1, 2026. Applies to all employers regardless of size. Sacramento does not have a local minimum wage above the state rate. Industry-specific rates: fast food workers $20/hr, healthcare workers on track for $25/hr. California does not allow a tip credit — employers must pay full minimum wage before tips.

### Overtime
- **Category**: `overtime`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: 1.5x after 8 hrs/day or 40 hrs/week; 2x after 12 hrs/day; 7th consecutive day: 1.5x first 8 hrs, 2x after 8 hrs
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (codified in CA Labor Code §510)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Overtime FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/faq_overtime.htm)
- **Details**: California requires both daily AND weekly overtime, unlike federal law which only requires weekly. Time-and-a-half (1.5x) applies after 8 hours in a workday or 40 hours in a workweek. Double time (2x) applies after 12 hours in a workday and for hours over 8 on the 7th consecutive day worked. As of 2026, minimum overtime rate is $25.35/hr (1.5x of $16.90). Exempt salary threshold is $70,304/yr. Small employers (25 or fewer) have had full daily/weekly overtime since January 1, 2025.

### Sick Leave
- **Category**: `sick_leave`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: 40 hrs (5 days) per year; accrual: 1 hr per 30 hrs worked; cap: 80 hrs (10 days)
- **Effective Date**: 2024-01-01 (expanded from 24 hrs to 40 hrs)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Paid Sick Leave](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/paid_sick_leave.htm)
- **Details**: Employees accrue at least 1 hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked. Employers must allow at least 40 hours (5 days) of use per year. Accrual cap is 80 hours (10 days). Usable after 90th day of employment. Full 40 hours must be available by 200th day. Expanded uses effective 2025-2026: jury duty, witness appearance, and crime victim support for employees and family members. No employer size exemption — applies to all employers.

### Leave
- **Category**: `leave`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: CFRA: 12 weeks unpaid job-protected leave; PFL: up to 8 weeks wage replacement (60-70% of wages, max $1,765/week in 2026); PDL: up to 4 months pregnancy disability leave
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing; PFL max benefit updated 2026-01-01
- **Source**: [EDD Paid Family Leave](https://edd.ca.gov/en/disability/paid-family-leave/); [EDD FMLA/CFRA FAQ](https://edd.ca.gov/en/disability/faqs-fmla-cfra/)
- **Details**: California has multiple overlapping leave programs. **CFRA** (California Family Rights Act): 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected leave for bonding, family care, or own serious health condition. Applies to employers with 5+ employees. **PFL** (Paid Family Leave): Up to 8 weeks wage replacement — 60-70% of wages depending on income (lower earners get 90%). Max weekly benefit $1,765 in 2026. Funded by employee SDI contributions, not job-protected on its own. **PDL** (Pregnancy Disability Leave): Up to 4 months for pregnancy-related disability, any employer size. SB 590 (effective July 2028) will expand PFL to cover "designated persons."

### Meal & Rest Breaks
- **Category**: `meal_breaks`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: 30-min unpaid meal break before end of 5th hour; 2nd meal break before end of 10th hour; 10-min paid rest break per 4 hrs worked
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Labor Code §§226.7, 512)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Meal Periods FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/faq_mealperiods.htm); [CalChamber Meal and Rest Breaks](https://www.calchamber.com/california-labor-law/meal-and-rest-breaks)
- **Details**: **Meal breaks**: 30-minute unpaid off-duty meal break no later than end of 5th hour. Second 30-min meal break required if working more than 10 hours (may be waived if total ≤12 hrs and first wasn't waived). First meal break waivable only if total ≤6 hrs. **Rest breaks**: Paid 10-minute rest period for every 4 hours worked (or major fraction thereof), starting at 3.5 hours. Cannot be waived. **Penalties**: 1 hour of pay at regular rate for each missed meal or rest break per workday.

### Final Pay
- **Category**: `final_pay`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Termination: immediately; Quit with 72hr notice: at time of quitting; Quit without notice: within 72 hrs
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Labor Code §§201-203)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Final Pay](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/finalpay.pdf); [CA DIR Paydays FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/faq_paydays.htm)
- **Details**: **Involuntary termination**: All wages including accrued vacation due immediately at time of termination. **Voluntary quit with ≥72 hrs notice**: Due at time of quitting. **Voluntary quit without notice**: Due within 72 hours; employee may request mailing. Final pay must include all earned wages, accrued vacation, unpaid overtime, and owed bonuses/commissions. **Waiting time penalty**: Employee's daily rate of pay for each day wages remain unpaid, up to 30 calendar days maximum.

### Pay Frequency
- **Category**: `pay_frequency`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Semi-monthly minimum; wages earned 1st-15th due by 26th; wages earned 16th-last day due by 10th of following month
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Labor Code §§204-210)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Paydays FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/faq_paydays.htm)
- **Details**: Most employees must be paid at least twice per calendar month on designated paydays. Wages earned between 1st-15th due no later than the 26th; wages earned 16th-last day due by 10th of the following month. Weekly/biweekly pay periods allowed — must pay within 7 calendar days of period end. Exempt (executive/administrative/professional) employees may be paid monthly by 26th. Agricultural workers must be paid at least monthly. Employer must post payday notice.

### Scheduling & Reporting Time
- **Category**: `scheduling_reporting`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Reporting time pay: minimum 2 hrs pay (max 4 hrs) if employee reports and is sent home; split shift premium: 1 hr at minimum wage
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (IWC Wage Orders)
- **Source**: [CA Employment Law Report - Scheduling](https://www.californiaemploymentlawreport.com/2025/07/five-legal-realities-of-employee-scheduling-in-california/)
- **Details**: **No statewide predictive scheduling law** — but local ordinances exist in San Francisco, Emeryville, San Jose, and Los Angeles (not Sacramento). **Reporting time pay**: If employee reports to work and is not provided at least half their usual/scheduled day, they must be paid for at least half the day — minimum 2 hours, maximum 4 hours at regular rate. **Split shift premium**: When work schedule is interrupted by unpaid break, employees are entitled to 1 hour at minimum wage as premium. Sacramento does not have a local predictive scheduling ordinance.

### Workers' Comp
- **Category**: `workers_comp`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Mandatory for all employers from first employee; max TTD $1,764.11/week (2026)
- **Effective Date**: 2026-01-01 (benefit rates updated annually)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Workers' Comp FAQ](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dwc/faqs.html); [CA DIR Employer Info](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dwc/employer.htm)
- **Details**: California requires all employers to carry workers' compensation insurance from the moment they hire their first employee — no minimum employee threshold. Coverage available through licensed insurers or State Compensation Insurance Fund. Self-insurance option available. **2026 rates**: Max temporary total disability (TTD) $1,764.11/week. Industry assessment factor ~5.191% of premiums. **Penalties for non-compliance**: Misdemeanor, up to $100,000 in civil penalties, jail time, mandatory business stop orders. Licensed contractors in high-risk categories must carry coverage even with no employees.

### Workplace Safety
- **Category**: `workplace_safety`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Cal/OSHA state plan — standards at least as effective as federal OSHA, often stricter
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing
- **Source**: [Cal/OSHA Home](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dosh/); [Federal OSHA CA State Plan](https://www.osha.gov/stateplans/ca)
- **Details**: California operates its own OSHA-approved state plan (Cal/OSHA) covering all private-sector and state/local government employers. Cal/OSHA standards are often stricter than federal. **Key requirements**: Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP) mandatory for all employers. Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (SB 553, Labor Code §6401.9) required. Heat illness prevention program required for outdoor/hot industries. **Reporting**: Serious injuries/fatalities must be reported to Cal/OSHA within 8 hours; hospitalization of 3+ employees reported immediately. PELs for air contaminants updated more than 20 times since 1970 (vs. federal unchanged since 1970).

### Anti-Discrimination
- **Category**: `anti_discrimination`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: FEHA — 18+ protected classes; applies to employers with 5+ employees; enforced by Civil Rights Department (CRD)
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing; intersectionality amendment (SB 1137) effective 2025
- **Source**: [CA Civil Rights Department - Employment](https://calcivilrights.ca.gov/employment/)
- **Details**: California's Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) is among the broadest anti-discrimination laws in the US. **Protected classes**: race, color, religion, national origin, ancestry, physical disability, mental disability, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, age (40+), sexual orientation, military/veteran status, reproductive health decision-making. **2025 update**: SB 1137 added "intersectionality" as an explicit basis for discrimination claims (combination of protected characteristics). Covers employers with 5+ employees (vs. federal Title VII threshold of 15). Enforced by the Civil Rights Department (CRD, formerly DFEH). Private right of action available.

### Minor Work Permits
- **Category**: `minor_work_permit`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Work permit required for all minors; permits expire 5 days after new school year starts
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Education Code; Labor Code)
- **Source**: [CA DIR Minors & Employment](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/dlse-cl.htm); [CA DIR Minor Summary Charts](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dlse/minorssummarycharts.pdf)
- **Details**: All minors need a work permit signed by parent/guardian. **Ages 14-15**: Max 3 hrs on school day, 8 hrs non-school day; 18 hrs/week during school, 40 hrs/week non-school; hours 7am-7pm (9pm June 1 – Labor Day). **Ages 16-17**: Max 8 hrs/day, 48 hrs/week, 6 days/week when school not in session; during school 4 hrs/day on school days. Work permits expire 5 days after start of each new school year and must be renewed. Minors must be paid at least the full state minimum wage ($16.90/hr in 2026). Entertainment industry has separate permit requirements.

## Healthcare

### HIPAA Privacy & Security
- **Category**: `hipaa_privacy`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: CMIA (Confidentiality of Medical Information Act) — exceeds HIPAA in multiple areas
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Civil Code §§56-56.37)
- **Source**: [HIPAA Journal - CA Law](https://www.hipaajournal.com/hipaa-california-law/); [AccountableHQ - CMIA Explained](https://www.accountablehq.com/post/california-confidentiality-of-medical-information-act)
- **Details**: California's CMIA goes significantly beyond HIPAA: **Broader coverage** — applies to most businesses maintaining medical info, not just HIPAA covered entities. **Stricter authorization** — more specific requirements for valid authorizations than HIPAA. **Private right of action** — patients can sue directly for negligent unauthorized disclosures (HIPAA has no private right of action). **Higher penalties** — up to $25,000 per patient per incident for violations. **Electronic records** — must automatically record and preserve any change or deletion of electronic medical info. **Breach notification** — third-party service providers must promptly notify both the healthcare provider and affected patients. Also subject to CCPA for non-HIPAA health data.

### Clinical & Patient Safety
- **Category**: `clinical_safety`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Mandatory adverse event reporting to CDPH within 5 days (24 hrs for urgent/emergent events)
- **Effective Date**: 2022-01-01 (current regulations)
- **Source**: [CDPH Reportable Adverse Events](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CHCQ/LCP/Pages/Reportable-Adverse-Events.aspx)
- **Details**: California Department of Public Health (CDPH) requires hospitals to report adverse events through a secure electronic portal. **Timelines**: 5 calendar days for standard adverse events; 24 hours for urgent/emergent events threatening patient/staff safety or involving sexual assault. **Reportable events include**: wrong-site/wrong-patient surgery, retained foreign objects, deaths during anesthesia, medication errors causing death/serious disability. **Hospital requirements**: Must develop policies for internal reporting, conduct root cause analyses, and assess culture of safety at least every 24 months. Applies to general acute care hospitals and acute psychiatric hospitals.

### Billing & Financial Integrity
- **Category**: `billing_integrity`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: California False Claims Act (Gov Code §12650+); Medi-Cal fraud enforcement; AB 72 surprise billing protections
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing
- **Source**: [CA AG Medi-Cal Fraud](https://oag.ca.gov/dmfea/medical); [CA AG Fraud Laws](https://oag.ca.gov/dmfea/laws/crim_fraud)
- **Details**: **California False Claims Act**: Mirrors federal FCA with qui tam (whistleblower) provisions. Treble damages plus up to $22,000 per false claim. **Medi-Cal fraud enforcement**: Division of Medi-Cal Fraud and Elder Abuse (DMFEA) under the Attorney General investigates fraud — estimated billions per year. Criminal penalties under Penal Code §550 for healthcare fraud (billing for services not provided, unnecessary services, kickbacks). **Surprise billing**: AB 72 protects patients from balance billing by out-of-network providers at in-network facilities. Patients pay only in-network cost-sharing; billing disputes resolved between provider and insurer.

### Corporate Integrity & Ethics
- **Category**: `corporate_integrity`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Mandatory compliance programs for pharmaceutical companies per CA Health & Safety Code §§119400-119402
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing
- **Source**: [Venable - CA Compliance Law](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2004/10/new-california-law-mandates-compliance-with-oig-gu)
- **Details**: California requires pharmaceutical companies doing business in the state to: (1) Adopt a Comprehensive Compliance Program conforming to federal OIG's Compliance Program Guidance for Pharmaceutical Manufacturers, (2) Include policies complying with the PhRMA Code on interactions with healthcare professionals, (3) Make compliance program publicly available, (4) File annual written declaration of adherence. Must include annual aggregate dollar limits on gifts/promotional items to healthcare professionals. No explicit penalty provision in the statute, but other code sections provide enforcement mechanisms. Broader healthcare corporate integrity requirements follow federal OIG guidance.

### Emergency Preparedness
- **Category**: `emergency_preparedness`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state and federal
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: CMS Emergency Preparedness Rule + California Title 22 hospital regulations
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing
- **Source**: [CMS Emergency Preparedness Rule](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/quality-safety-oversight-emergency-preparedness/emergency-preparedness-rule); [CalHospitalPrepare Standards](https://www.calhospitalprepare.org/standards-regulations-statutes)
- **Details**: California hospitals must comply with both federal CMS and state Title 22 requirements. **CMS requirements**: Emergency plan based on facility/community risk assessment (all-hazards approach), updated every 2 years; communication plan; policies and procedures; annual training and exercises/drills. **California-specific**: Title 22 regulations address emergency service requirements, organizational standards, and seismic safety (OSHPD/HCAi building standards). California hospitals face unique risks (earthquakes, wildfires) requiring specific preparedness plans. Hospital Preparedness Program (HPP) provides additional healthcare coalition requirements.

### Healthcare Workforce
- **Category**: `healthcare_workforce`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Mandatory nurse-to-patient ratios (first state to mandate); mandatory overtime protections for nurses
- **Effective Date**: Ratios since 2004; SB 596 penalties effective 2026-01-01
- **Source**: [CA Workplace Law Blog - SB 596](https://www.californiaworkplacelawblog.com/2025/10/articles/california/california-sb-596-increases-penalties-for-healthcare-staffing-ratio-violations/)
- **Details**: California was the first state to mandate minimum nurse-to-patient staffing ratios. Ratios vary by unit type (e.g., 1:4 in specialty care, 1:2 in ICU). **SB 596** (effective January 1, 2026) raises maximum civil penalties for staffing ratio violations — each day out of compliance is a separate violation. **Mandatory overtime protections**: Nurses cannot be required to work mandatory overtime except in genuine emergencies (natural disasters). Cannot work over 72 hours/week even in emergencies. Nurses cannot be disciplined for refusing overtime. California also participates in the Nurse Licensure Compact for reciprocity.

### State Licensing & Scope
- **Category**: `state_licensing`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: CDPH licenses 11,000+ healthcare facilities; no Certificate of Need (CON) requirement
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing
- **Source**: [CDPH Licensing & Certification](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CHCQ/LCP/Pages/LandCProgramHome.aspx)
- **Details**: CDPH Licensing & Certification Program regulates 11,000+ facilities: general acute care hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, home health agencies, hospices, clinics, and more. Compliance evaluated against state (Title 22) and federal (CMS) standards. **No Certificate of Need**: California does NOT have a CON program — facilities can be built/expanded without state approval of need (unlike ~35 other states). **Scope of practice**: Licensed physicians required to have valid, unsuspended certificate. Home health agency licensing required since January 2016 (AB 1217). Primary care clinics licensed under Health & Safety Code §1200.

### Research & Informed Consent
- **Category**: `research_consent`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: California Protection of Human Subjects in Medical Experimentation Act + Experimental Subject's Bill of Rights
- **Effective Date**: Ongoing (CA Health & Safety Code §§24170-24179.5)
- **Source**: [CA AG Research Consent Guidelines](https://oag.ca.gov/research/consent); [CHHS Informed Consent Instructions](https://www.chhs.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/Informed-Consent-and-Assent-Instructions.pdf)
- **Details**: California exceeds federal Common Rule protections. **Experimental Subject's Bill of Rights**: Must be attached to the front of every informed consent form, signed and dated by participant before research begins. **Attestation requirement**: Consent form must be signed by a witness (other than participant or guardian) attesting informed consent requirements were met. **No exculpatory language**: Consent forms cannot include waivers of legal rights or releases from liability for negligence. **Surrogate consent**: Only permitted by IRB for research involving cognitive impairment or serious/life-threatening conditions. **Free choice**: Must be exercised without undue inducement, force, fraud, deceit, or coercion.

## Oncology

### Radiation Safety
- **Category**: `radiation_safety`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: NRC Agreement State since 1962; Radiologic Health Branch (CDPH) licenses and inspects all radioactive materials users
- **Effective Date**: Agreement since 1962; ongoing regulation
- **Source**: [NRC - California Agreement State Info](https://www.nrc.gov/agreement-states/california); [CDPH Radiologic Health Branch](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CEH/DRSEM/Pages/RHB-LawsAndRegs.aspx)
- **Details**: California is an NRC Agreement State, meaning the state has primary responsibility for regulating possession and use of radioactive materials. Administered by the Radiologic Health Branch (RHB) of CDPH. **Radiation Control Law** (Health & Safety Code §§114960-115273) requires licensing and regulation of all radioactive materials. RHB registers, licenses, and inspects users in medicine, academia, research, and industry. NRC evaluates California's program every 3-4 years for adequacy and compatibility. State standards under Title 17 CCR §30253+ for radiation protection.

### Chemotherapy & Hazardous Drugs
- **Category**: `chemotherapy_handling`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state and federal
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: USP <800> enforceable by CA Board of Pharmacy; Cal/OSHA hazardous drug exposure standards
- **Effective Date**: USP <800> enforceable since 2023-11-01
- **Source**: [USP <800> Standards](https://www.usp.org/compounding/general-chapter-hazardous-drugs-handling-healthcare)
- **Details**: **USP <800>**: Enforceable by California Board of Pharmacy since November 2023. Covers receipt, storage, compounding, dispensing, administration, and disposal of hazardous drugs. **Requirements**: Negative pressure room for HD compounding, 12 air changes per hour for HD storage, double chemotherapy gloves + full PPE for compounding, training before independent handling with annual reassessment. **Cal/OSHA**: Additional workplace exposure standards for hazardous drugs. California considers altering the state of a product (including adding flavoring) as compounding, triggering stricter requirements. All facilities handling chemotherapy must maintain hazardous drug lists, exposure assessments, and spill management procedures.

### Tumor Registry Reporting
- **Category**: `tumor_registry`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Mandatory cancer reporting to California Cancer Registry (CCR) since 1985; physicians must report within 6 months
- **Effective Date**: 1985 (law enacted); electronic reporting required since 2019
- **Source**: [California Cancer Registry](https://www.ccrcal.org/learn-about-ccr/); [CCR Data Standards](https://www.ccrcal.org/submit-data/); [CCR Legislation](https://www.ccrcal.org/learn-about-ccr/about-cancer-registries/cancer-reporting-legislation/)
- **Details**: Cancer reporting has been required by California law since 1985. **Who must report**: All hospitals, facilities, physicians, and pathologists diagnosing or treating cancer patients. **Deadlines**: Physicians must report within 6 months of initial diagnosis. Regional registries report annually within 12 months of calendar year close. Pathologists must report electronically (required since January 2019 per AB 2325). **Data required**: Demographic, diagnostic, and treatment data. Reports submitted through California Cancer Reporting Portal. CCR is part of the National Program of Cancer Registries (CDC) and SEER (NCI).

### Oncology Clinical Trials
- **Category**: `oncology_clinical_trials`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: SB 37 mandates insurance coverage of routine patient care costs in cancer clinical trials; Right to Try law for experimental treatments
- **Effective Date**: SB 37 signed August 2001, effective 2002-01-01
- **Source**: [CA Legislature SB-37](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=200120020SB37); [DMHC Clinical Trials](https://www.dmhc.ca.gov/HealthCareinCalifornia/GettheBestCare/ClinicalTrials.aspx)
- **Details**: **SB 37 (Insurance Coverage Mandate)**: Health plans must cover routine patient care costs for cancer clinical trials approved by NIH, FDA, DoD, or VA (Phases I-IV). Plans cannot discriminate against enrollees for participating in approved trials. Covers routine costs like lab work, imaging, and office visits — not the experimental agent itself. **Right to Try**: Terminally ill patients can access drugs not fully FDA-approved, outside of clinical trials. **SB 583**: Extended clinical trial protections and access. Despite mandates, insurance denials remain a barrier to trial enrollment in practice.

### Oncology Patient Rights
- **Category**: `oncology_patient_rights`
- **Jurisdiction Level**: state
- **Jurisdiction Name**: California
- **Current Value**: Cancer Patients' Bill of Rights (SCR-11, 2021) — first state legislation of its kind in the US
- **Effective Date**: 2021-08-30
- **Source**: [CA Legislature SCR-11](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202120220SCR11); [City of Hope - Cancer Patients' Bill of Rights](https://www.cityofhope.org/breakthroughs/california-assembly-approves-cancer-patients-bill-of-rights)
- **Details**: California adopted the first Cancer Patients' Bill of Rights in the US (SCR-11, 2021). **Six key rights**: (1) Right to understand diagnosis and be informed of treatment options in culturally appropriate language. (2) Right to transparent, timely access to oncology specialists and diagnostic testing. (3) Right to cancer subspecialists with expertise in their specific cancer subtype. (4) Right to pain management and supportive services. (5) Right to second opinions, palliative care, multidisciplinary expertise, and clinical trial participation. (6) Right to access NCI-Designated Comprehensive Cancer Centers for complex cancers requiring emerging therapies or precision medicine. General patient rights (Title 22 §70707) also guarantee right to consent to or refuse any treatment.
